# CodeGuard Investor Deck Outline

## Positioning

CodeGuard is the independent audit firm for the age of AI-built software.
The analogy is not "another developer tool"; it is McKinsey / Big Four discipline applied to AI-heavy codebases.

Core thesis:
- Microsoft, GitHub, Cursor, and similar vendors are code factories.
- Code factories are economically rewarded when more code is generated.
- AI is the dominant venture-capital theme, and AI coding is one of the clearest application-layer wedges inside that theme.
- A direct competitor to Codex, Claude Code, Copilot, or Cursor is a poor venture entry point because the giants own models, distribution, and developer workflow surface area.
- The market therefore needs an independent arbiter whose incentive is not more generation, but lower architectural risk.
- CodeGuard sells architectural discipline as an asset: expert judgment, AI-exoskeleton throughput, Project Guardrails, and management-grade reporting.

Tone:
- adult-to-adult
- sober and authoritative
- risk, compliance, audit, standards, governance
- no "we will conquer the world with AI" language
- margin from chaos, not hype

## Unit Economics Target

These numbers are investor-deck targets, not audited operating history.

Base case:
- Initial audit price: `$25k`
- Architect effort with internal tooling: `12-16 hours`
- Fully-loaded internal COGS: `$3k-5k`
- Audit gross margin target: `80-85%`
- Retainer price: `$10k/month`
- Retainer effort after guardrails: `6-10 hours/month`
- Retainer gross margin target: `65-80%`
- Year-1 LTV target: `$145k` = `$25k audit + 12 x $10k retainer`

Enterprise case:
- Initial audit: `$50k`
- Retainer: `$20k/month`
- Year-1 revenue: `$290k`

Traditional audit proxy:
- `2 senior architects x 20 days x 6 billable hours x $200/hour = ~$48k`
- CodeGuard target COGS for equivalent first-map output: `~$4k`
- Narrative: the client buys a cheaper, faster audit outcome; CodeGuard keeps consulting-level pricing with software-like leverage.

## Market Timing Evidence

Use these as sourced market anchors, not as proof of CodeGuard revenue:

- AI firms captured `61%` of global VC investment in 2025, equal to `$258.7B` of `$427.1B` total VC deal value, according to OECD.
- Stanford AI Index 2026 reports `$170.9B` in global private investment in generative AI in 2025.
- Cursor disclosed three 2025 rounds on its own blog: `$105M` Series B in January, `$900M` Series C in June, and `$2.3B` Series D in November, totaling `$3.305B` in one year.
- Cognition disclosed a further `$400M` round in September 2025 at a `$10.2B` valuation.
- That gives a public 2025 floor of `$3.7B+` into AI coding leaders from just Cursor and Cognition alone, before counting undisclosed rounds, acquisitions, or private transactions.
- GitHub Octoverse 2025 reports `180M+` developers on GitHub, `4.3M` AI-related repositories, `80%` of new developers using Copilot within their first week, and `+178% YoY` growth in public repositories importing LLM SDKs.
- JetBrains AI Pulse January 2026 reports `90%` of developers regularly used at least one AI tool at work for coding and development tasks, and `74%` had adopted specialized AI developer tools such as coding assistants, AI editors, and agents.
- Stack Overflow 2025 reports `84%` of respondents use or plan to use AI tools in development, while trust and sentiment have declined. This is the demand/control gap CodeGuard monetizes.

Investment inference:
- The AI capital wave is already obvious.
- AI coding is already commercially validated.
- Public data does not yet provide a clean global breakout of the full `$170.9B` GenAI investment pool by software subcategory.
- But a disclosed `$3.7B+` floor into AI coding leaders is already large enough to prove serious capital concentration.
- Direct code-factory competition is overexposed to giants.
- The investable gap is the independent control layer above AI-generated code.

## Bottom-Up Market Model

Do not present a fake top-down TAM for "AI governance."
Use an assumption-driven service market model instead.

Inputs:
- GitHub reports `4M+` organizations and `180M+` developers on the platform.
- Stack Overflow reports `84%` of developers use or plan to use AI tools.
- JetBrains reports `74%` of developers have already adopted specialized AI developer tools.
- CodeGuard target year-1 ACV: `$145k` (`$25k audit + 12 x $10k retainer`).

Sensitivity model:
- `0.25%` of GitHub organizations = `10k` potential buyers -> `~$1.45B`
- `0.5%` of GitHub organizations = `20k` potential buyers -> `~$2.9B`
- `1.0%` of GitHub organizations = `40k` potential buyers -> `~$5.8B`

Why this framing works:
- It does not pretend every developer team is a buyer.
- It frames CodeGuard as a premium service sold to a small, high-risk, high-value slice of the market.
- It matches the moat thesis: limited expert throughput can still support a very large business.

## Narrative Spine

1. AI coding tools are creating a new asset-quality problem.
2. AI coding is the clearest application-layer wedge, but direct competition is a giants' game.
3. The problem is not code volume alone; it is loss of architectural understanding.
4. Code-factory vendors cannot credibly audit their own output.
5. The missing category is independent architectural arbitration.
6. CodeGuard combines human authority with AI-exoskeleton throughput.
7. The initial audit creates the trust wedge.
8. Project Guardrails turn findings into reusable project infrastructure.
9. Retainers monetize immunity while marginal expert effort declines.
10. The moat is controlled expert capacity, methodology, brand trust, and accountability.
11. CodeGuard can become the de facto audit standard for AI-built software.

### Slide 3. The Inevitable Mess

Punchline:
`AI made code generation cheap. Architectural understanding did not get cheaper.`

- Codebases can grow faster than human review capacity.
- The hidden risk is cognitive debt: the company owns software no one fully understands.

### Slide 4. Why This Is An Investment Risk

Punchline:
`Cognitive debt turns engineering chaos into asset risk.`

Core points:
- Due diligence gets weaker when the codebase looks functional but lacks explainability.
- Key-person risk rises when only one developer or AI session history explains the system.
- Burn increases when AI speed creates rework, hidden dependencies, and brittle architecture.

Visual:
- Risk chain: `AI output -> architecture drift -> cognitive debt -> slower delivery -> valuation discount`.
- Use valuation / diligence language, not developer productivity language.
